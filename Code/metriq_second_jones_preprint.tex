\begin{abstract}
For a summable positive sequence $x=(x_k)$, its achievement set is the compact set of all subsums
\[
  \E(x)=\left\{\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\varepsilon_kx_k:\varepsilon_k\in\{0,1\}\right\}.
\]
The Second Jones Problem asks whether there is a sequence for which the consecutive-term ratio has a limit while the achievement set is a Cantorval. We give the explicit construction
\[
  x_{2n-1}=\frac{8n+17}{4^n},
  \qquad
  x_{2n}=\frac{4n+18}{4^n}
  \qquad(n\ge 1).
\]
We prove that $x$ is strictly decreasing and summable, that
$\lim_{k\to\infty}x_{k+1}/x_k=1/2$, that $\E(x)$ has nonempty interior, and that $\E(x)$ has infinitely many gaps. The interior argument is driven by the fact that the four pair-digits form a complete residue system modulo $4$ at every scale. The gaps follow from an exact tail identity. The Guthrie--Nymann classification theorem, with the corrected proof of Nymann and S\'aenz, then implies that $\E(x)$ is a Cantorval. Subject to independent verification, this gives an affirmative answer to the Second Jones Problem.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Let $x=(x_k)_{k\ge1}$ be an absolutely summable real sequence. Its \emph{achievement set}, also called its set of subsums, is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:achievement-set}
  \E(x)
  :=
  \left\{
    \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\varepsilon_kx_k:
    (\varepsilon_k)_{k\ge1}\in\{0,1\}^{\N}
  \right\}.
\end{equation}
The systematic study of such sets goes back to Kakeya \cite{Kakeya1914}. For an infinite positive summable sequence, $\E(x)$ is compact and perfect. The one-dimensional classification theorem of Guthrie and Nymann \cite{GuthrieNymann1988}, whose proof was corrected by Nymann and S\'aenz \cite{NymannSaenz2000}, states that an achievement set is one of four topological types: a finite set, a finite union of closed bounded intervals, a Cantor set, or a Cantorval.

Jones \cite[p.~519]{Jones2011} asked whether a sequence can simultaneously have a convergent adjacent-term ratio and a Cantorval achievement set. A recent survey records the question as follows \cite[Problem~4]{GlabPrus2025}:
\begin{quote}
Does there exist a sequence $(x_n)$ such that $\lim_{n\to\infty}x_{n+1}/x_n$ exists and $\E(x_n)$ is a Cantorval?
\end{quote}
Known Cantorval constructions are often multigeometric, and their periodic block structure usually prevents the full consecutive-term ratio from converging unless the block degenerates. The construction in this paper replaces fixed block coefficients with slowly varying linear coefficients. This preserves the base-$4$ digit mechanism that creates interior while making both types of adjacent ratios converge to the same value.

The proof has two independent geometric components. First, the four possible subsums from the $n$th pair represent all residue classes modulo $4$. A finite-union category argument then forces $\E(x)$ to contain an interval. Second, the even term $x_{2n}$ exceeds the entire remaining tail by exactly $4^{-n}$, producing a gap at every scale. These two facts exclude both the Cantor-set and multi-interval alternatives in the classification theorem.

\section{Preliminaries}

Throughout, $\N=\{1,2,3,\ldots\}$. For a positive summable sequence $x$, write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:remainder}
  r_k:=\sum_{j=k+1}^{\infty}x_j
\end{equation}
for the remainder after the $k$th term.

\begin{definition}
A \emph{multi-interval set} is a finite union of bounded closed intervals. A \emph{Cantor set} is a set homeomorphic to the classical ternary Cantor set. A \emph{Cantorval} is the fourth nonfinite topological type in the Guthrie--Nymann classification; equivalently, it is homeomorphic to the model achievement set generated by
\[
  \frac{3}{4},\frac{2}{4},\frac{3}{4^2},\frac{2}{4^2},\ldots.
\]
See \cite{GuthrieNymann1988,NymannSaenz2000,MendesOliveira1994}.
\end{definition}

We shall use only the following consequence of the classification theorem.

\begin{proposition}[Classification consequence]\label{prop:classification-consequence}
Let $x$ be an infinite positive summable sequence. If $\E(x)$ has nonempty interior and infinitely many gaps, then $\E(x)$ is a Cantorval.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Nonempty interior rules out a Cantor set. Infinitely many gaps rule out a finite union of closed intervals. The set is not finite because $x$ has infinitely many positive terms. The classification theorem leaves only the Cantorval alternative.
\end{proof}

\section{The construction and main result}

Define, for every $n\ge1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:construction}
  a_n:=\frac{8n+17}{4^n},
  \qquad
  b_n:=\frac{4n+18}{4^n},
\end{equation}
and interlace the two sequences by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:interlace}
  x_{2n-1}=a_n,
  \qquad
  x_{2n}=b_n.
\end{equation}
The first terms are
\[
  \frac{25}{4},\ \frac{22}{4},\ \frac{33}{16},\ \frac{26}{16},\
  \frac{41}{64},\ \frac{30}{64},\ldots.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem]\label{thm:main}
The sequence defined by \eqref{eq:construction}--\eqref{eq:interlace} is positive, strictly decreasing, and satisfies
\[
  \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}x_k=17,
  \qquad
  \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{x_{k+1}}{x_k}=\frac12.
\]
Its achievement set $\E(x)$ has nonempty interior and infinitely many gaps. Consequently, $\E(x)$ is a Cantorval.
\end{theorem}

The constants in \eqref{eq:construction} meet three requirements at once:

\]

\section{Elementary properties and the ratio limit}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:monotone-sum}
The sequence $x$ is positive and strictly decreasing, and its total sum is $17$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Positivity is immediate. Within the $n$th pair,
\[
  x_{2n-1}-x_{2n}
  =\frac{(8n+17)-(4n+18)}{4^n}
  =\frac{4n-1}{4^n}>0.
\]
Between successive pairs,
\begin{align*}
  x_{2n}-x_{2n+1}
  &=\frac{4n+18}{4^n}-\frac{8(n+1)+17}{4^{n+1}}\\
  &=\frac{16n+72-(8n+25)}{4^{n+1}}\\
  &=\frac{8n+47}{4^{n+1}}>0.
\end{align*}
Thus $x_1>x_2>x_3>\cdots>0$.

The sum of the $n$th pair is
\[
  x_{2n-1}+x_{2n}=\frac{12n+35}{4^n}.
\]
Using
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{4^n}=\frac{4}{9},
  \qquad
  \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{4^n}=\frac13,
\]
we obtain
\[
  \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}x_k
  =12\left(\frac49\right)+35\left(\frac13\right)
  =17.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ratio}
The consecutive-term ratio converges to $1/2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For adjacent terms within one pair,
\[
  \frac{x_{2n}}{x_{2n-1}}
  =\frac{4n+18}{8n+17}
  \longrightarrow\frac12.
\]
For the transition from one pair to the next,
\[
  \frac{x_{2n+1}}{x_{2n}}
  =\frac{(8n+25)/4^{n+1}}{(4n+18)/4^n}
  =\frac{8n+25}{16n+72}
  \longrightarrow\frac12.
\]
Every adjacent ratio belongs to one of these two subsequences, so the full sequence converges to $1/2$.
\end{proof}

\section{A complete residue system forces interior}

Group the terms into pairs. From the $n$th pair, a subsum contributes one of the four quantities
\[
  0,
  \qquad
  \frac{4n+18}{4^n},
  \qquad
  \frac{8n+17}{4^n},
  \qquad
  \frac{12n+35}{4^n}.
\]
Define the integer digit set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:digits}
  D_n:=\{0,\ 4n+18,\ 8n+17,\ 12n+35\}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:digit-representation}
  \E(x)
  =
  \left\{
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{d_n}{4^n}:d_n\in D_n
  \right\}.
\end{equation}
Modulo $4$, the elements of $D_n$ are respectively
\[
  0,\quad2,\quad1,\quad3.
\]
Hence $D_n$ contains exactly one representative of each residue class modulo $4$.

\begin{lemma}[Mixed-radix residue lemma]\label{lem:residues}
Fix $N\ge1$. For $d_n\in D_n$, define
\[
  P_N(d_1,\ldots,d_N)
  :=\sum_{n=1}^{N}d_n4^{N-n}.
\]
As $(d_1,\ldots,d_N)$ ranges over $D_1\times\cdots\times D_N$, the integers $P_N(d_1,\ldots,d_N)$ represent every residue class modulo $4^N$ exactly once.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
There are $4^N$ digit strings, so it is enough to prove injectivity modulo $4^N$. Suppose
\[
  P_N(d_1,\ldots,d_N)
  \equiv
  P_N(d'_1,\ldots,d'_N)
  \pmod{4^N}.
\]
Reducing modulo $4$ gives $d_N\equiv d'_N\pmod4$. Because $D_N$ contains one representative of each residue class modulo $4$, we have $d_N=d'_N$. Subtract the common last digit and divide the congruence by $4$. The same argument now applies modulo $4^{N-1}$ to the first $N-1$ digits. Iteration yields $d_n=d'_n$ for every $n\le N$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:interior}
The achievement set $\E(x)$ has nonempty interior.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix $N\ge1$ and an integer $r$ with $0\le r<4^N$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:residues}, there is a digit string $(d_1,\ldots,d_N)$ such that
\[
  P_N(d_1,\ldots,d_N)=r+m4^N
\]
for some integer $m$. Dividing by $4^N$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:grid-translate}
  \sum_{n=1}^{N}\frac{d_n}{4^n}
  =m+\frac{r}{4^N}.
\end{equation}
The left side is a finite subsum, hence an element of $\E(x)$. It is nonnegative and strictly less than the total sum $17$. Since $0\le r/4^N<1$, it follows that
\[
  m\in\{0,1,\ldots,16\}.
\]
Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:grid-cover}
  \left\{\frac{r}{4^N}:0\le r<4^N\right\}
  \subseteq
  \bigcup_{m=0}^{16}\bigl(\E(x)-m\bigr).
\end{equation}
Taking the union over $N$, the left side is dense in $[0,1]$. The set $\E(x)$ is compact: it is the continuous image of the compact product space $\{0,1\}^{\N}$ under the uniformly convergent subsum map. Hence the finite union
\[
  F:=\bigcup_{m=0}^{16}\bigl(\E(x)-m\bigr)
\]
is closed. Equation \eqref{eq:grid-cover} therefore implies $[0,1]\subseteq F$.

Assume for contradiction that $\operatorname{int}\E(x)=\varnothing$. Because $\E(x)$ is closed, it is nowhere dense; every translate $\E(x)-m$ is likewise nowhere dense. A finite union of nowhere-dense sets is nowhere dense and cannot contain the interval $[0,1]$. This contradicts $[0,1]\subseteq F$. Therefore $\operatorname{int}\E(x)\ne\varnothing$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof does not locate a particular interval inside $\E(x)$. Instead, the complete residue systems show that finitely many integer translates of $\E(x)$ cover a dense base-$4$ grid; compactness and category force one translate, and therefore $\E(x)$ itself, to contain an interval.
\end{remark}

\section{An exact gap at every scale}

\begin{lemma}[Tail identity]\label{lem:tail}
For every $n\ge1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tail-identity}
  r_{2n}=\sum_{k=2n+1}^{\infty}x_k=\frac{4n+17}{4^n}.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-difference}
  x_{2n}-r_{2n}=\frac{1}{4^n}>0.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Grouping all later terms into pairs gives
\[
  r_{2n}
  =\sum_{j=n+1}^{\infty}\frac{12j+35}{4^j}.
\]
The geometric-series identities
\[
  \sum_{j=n+1}^{\infty}\frac1{4^j}=\frac{1}{3\cdot4^n},
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=n+1}^{\infty}\frac{j}{4^j}=\frac{3n+4}{9\cdot4^n}
\]
yield
\begin{align*}
  r_{2n}
  &=12\frac{3n+4}{9\cdot4^n}
    +35\frac{1}{3\cdot4^n}\\
  &=\frac{4n+17}{4^n}.
\end{align*}
Since $x_{2n}=(4n+18)/4^n$, equation \eqref{eq:gap-difference} follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:gaps}
For every $n\ge1$, the open interval
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap}
  G_n
  :=
  \left(
    \frac{4n+17}{4^n},
    \frac{4n+18}{4^n}
  \right)
  =(r_{2n},x_{2n})
\end{equation}
is a gap of $\E(x)$. The gaps $G_n$ are pairwise distinct.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Both endpoints of $G_n$ belong to $\E(x)$. The left endpoint $r_{2n}$ is obtained by selecting every term after $x_{2n}$ and no earlier term. The right endpoint $x_{2n}$ is obtained by selecting only $x_{2n}$.

Now take any subsum. If it uses none of the first $2n$ terms, it is at most $r_{2n}$. If it uses at least one of the first $2n$ terms, then it is at least $x_{2n}$, because the sequence is decreasing and $x_{2n}$ is the smallest of those first $2n$ terms. Hence no subsum lies strictly between $r_{2n}$ and $x_{2n}$, proving $G_n\cap\E(x)=\varnothing$.

Finally,
\[
  x_{2n+2}
  =\frac{4n+22}{4^{n+1}}
  <\frac{16n+68}{4^{n+1}}
  =r_{2n}.
\]
Thus $G_{n+1}$ lies strictly to the left of $G_n$, so the gaps are pairwise distinct.
\end{proof}

\section{Classification and resolution of the problem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:main}]
The elementary properties and total sum are established in Lemma~\ref{lem:monotone-sum}. The ratio limit is \cref{prop:ratio}. The achievement set has nonempty interior by \cref{prop:interior} and infinitely many gaps by \cref{prop:gaps}. Applying \cref{prop:classification-consequence}, we conclude that $\E(x)$ is a Cantorval.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Normalized form]\label{cor:normalized}
Let $y_k=x_k/17$. Then
\[
  \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}y_k=1,
  \qquad
  \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{y_{k+1}}{y_k}=\frac12,
\]
and $\E(y)=\frac1{17}\E(x)$ is a Cantorval.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Positive scaling preserves adjacent ratios and topological type, and scales the total sum and achievement set by the same factor.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Answer to the Second Jones Problem]
The sequence in \eqref{eq:construction}--\eqref{eq:interlace} has a convergent consecutive-term ratio and a Cantorval achievement set. Accordingly, the theorem gives an affirmative answer to the Second Jones Problem as formulated by Jones \cite[p.~519]{Jones2011} and restated in \cite[Problem~4]{GlabPrus2025}, subject to independent verification of this manuscript.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}

The construction is not multigeometric: the coefficients within each base-$4$ block vary with $n$. It nevertheless retains the decisive arithmetic feature of the Guthrie--Nymann model, namely a full set of residues at every base-$4$ digit position. This suggests a broader design principle.

Suppose a block construction in base $q$ has finite digit sets $D_n\subset\N$ that are complete residue systems modulo $q$, while all finite partial sums remain in a fixed bounded interval. Then a variant of Lemma~\ref{lem:residues} and Proposition~\ref{prop:interior} can force interior through finitely many translates. If, independently, selected block terms exceed their following tails infinitely often, the same construction may generate infinitely many gaps. Slowly varying block coefficients can then be tuned so that distinct adjacent-ratio subsequences converge to a common limit.

The present paper does not attempt a classification of such variable-block constructions. Two immediate directions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=0.25em,topsep=0.4em]
  \item determine parameter families that preserve the complete-residue and exact-gap identities; and
  \item identify the location, measure, and component structure of the interior of $\E(x)$ beyond the existence argument given here.
\end{enumerate}

No numerical approximation is needed for the proof. The only external structural input is the one-dimensional achievement-set classification theorem.
